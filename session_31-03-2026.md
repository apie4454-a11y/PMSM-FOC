## Motor Constants Derivation

### Calculating Flux Linkage from Kv

When manufacturers omit $\lambda_m$ (flux linkage), derive it from the speed constant $K_v$ using the fundamental PMSM relationship:

$$K_v^{DC} = \frac{RPM}{V} = \frac{2262}{16} = 141.4 \text{ RPM/V}$$

Then apply the inverse relationship for d-q motor model space:
$$\lambda_m = \frac{60}{2\pi \times P \times K_v} = \frac{60}{2\pi \times 11 \times 141.4} = 0.00611 \text{ Wb}$$

**Key Point:** The datasheet voltage (16V) is DC-equivalent. No √(3/2) conversion needed because the motor is inherently a DC device; the 3-phase comes from the inverter modulation, not the motor specification. The d-q motor model uses this λm directly.

**Transform Note:** The magnitude-invariant (amplitude-invariant) Park-Clarke transform uses 2/3 scaling: $T = \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}$ for coordinate transformation, and the d-q motor impedance is scaled by 3/2 relative to phase impedance.

## Torque Calculation & Design Selection

### Why Torque Varies: Manufacturing Tolerance in Magnet Strength

The datasheet specifies a torque range of **600–1000 g·cm (0.0588–0.0981 Nm)** at 1A. This variation is **NOT a control issue**—it reflects manufacturing tolerance in permanent magnet strength, which directly affects flux linkage (λm).

### Root Cause: Speed Range Implies Different Kv Values

The motor no-load speed range at 16V is **2149–2375 RPM**. Each of these motors has a different speed constant (Kv), implying different flux linkages:

**Slower Motor (Strong Magnets):**
$$K_v^{slow} = \frac{2149 \text{ RPM}}{16 \text{ V}} = 134.3 \text{ RPM/V}$$
$$\lambda_m^{slow} = \frac{60}{2\pi \cdot 11 \cdot 134.3} = 0.00641 \text{ Wb}$$
$$\tau_{slow} = 11 \times 0.00641 \times 1 = 0.0705 \text{ Nm} \quad (705 \text{ g·cm, upper range})$$

**Faster Motor (Weak Magnets):**
$$K_v^{fast} = \frac{2375 \text{ RPM}}{16 \text{ V}} = 148.4 \text{ RPM/V}$$
$$\lambda_m^{fast} = \frac{60}{2\pi \cdot 11 \cdot 148.4} = 0.00579 \text{ Wb}$$
$$\tau_{fast} = 11 \times 0.00579 \times 1 = 0.0637 \text{ Nm} \quad (637 \text{ g·cm, lower range})$$

**The Counterintuitive Physics:** A motor that spins faster has **weaker magnets**. Strong magnets create higher back-EMF, limiting no-load speed but producing more torque. Weak magnets allow higher speed but less torque. This inverse relationship explains the 600–1000 g·cm spread.

### Two Design Values: Which to Use?

| Design Phase | Parameter | Value | Why | Use Case |
|--------------|-----------|-------|-----|----------|
| **SIMULATION** | $\lambda_m$ | **0.00611 Wb** | Average case (midpoint: 2262 RPM @ 16V) | MATLAB/Simulink model, controller tuning |
| **REAL WORLD** | $\tau$ (datasheet) | **0.0934 Nm** | Conservative upper-bound, empirically measured | Mechanical design, load calc, margin planning |
| **WORST CASE** | $\tau$ (lower bound) | **0.0588 Nm** | Minimum you *might* get | Safety margin, never rely on this |

### Implementation Guidance

**For Simulation (MATLAB/Simulink):**
- Use $\lambda_m = 0.00611$ Wb (derived from average Kv = 141.4 RPM/V)
- This gives $\tau = 11 \times 0.00611 \times I = 0.0671$ Nm @ 1A in simulation
- Model represents a "typical" motor from the production batch
- Tune PI gains using this value

**For Hardware Inverted Pendulum:**
- Design mechanics assuming **0.0934 Nm** (datasheet conservative value)
- With 10:1 gearing: **0.934 Nm at wheel** (margin-inclusive)
- If motor under-performs in practice (you get 0.0638 Nm instead), you still have ~2.5% safety cushion
- If it over-performs (you get 0.0705 Nm), you have additional margin

**For Worst-Case Analysis:**
- Only cite 0.0588 Nm if designing for system reliability or automotive-grade certification
- Not necessary for hobby inverted pendulum projects

### Why They Differ: Theory vs. Practice

$$\lambda_m(\text{from Kv}) = \frac{60}{2\pi P K_v} \quad \text{(theoretical, inverses no-load speed)}$$

$$K_t(\text{from datasheet}) = \frac{\tau_{measured}}{I} \quad \text{(empirical, includes real motor losses and magnet variation)}$$

Our **0.00611 Wb** is derived from published Kv. The datasheet's **0.0934 Nm** is what they actually measure in production. Small discrepancies (7–15%) are normal and reflect:
- Copper losses and eddy currents under load
- Temperature effects during measurement
- Manufacturing spread in magnet grade
- Rotor assembly tolerances

## Voltage Budget Analysis

### Operating Point: 2262 RPM Average (No-Load Datasheet Range) at 1A Rated Load

The motor is rated for **12V DC nominal** (3S LiPo battery). At 2262 RPM with 1A load, the PMSM voltage equations reveal the true power budget:

#### Step 1: Calculate Electrical Frequency
$$\omega_e = \frac{2262 \times 2\pi \times P}{60} = \frac{2262 \times 2\pi \times 11}{60} \approx 2605.9 \text{ rad/s}$$

#### Step 2: Steady-State d-q Voltage Requirements

At **2262 RPM with 1A rated load** (using L_phase=2mH, L_dq=3mH, R_dq=8.4Ω):

**Using amplitude-invariant 2/3 transform:** $R_{dq} = 8.4$ Ω, $L_{dq} = 3$ mH

**q-axis voltage (Torque/Speed):**
$$V_q = (1.0 \times 8.4) + (2605.9 \times 0.00611) = 8.4 + 15.93 = \boxed{24.33 \text{ V}}$$

**d-axis voltage (Flux):**
$$V_d = -(2605.9 \times 0.003 \times 1.0) = \boxed{-7.82 \text{ V}}$$

**Torque at this operating point (2262 RPM @ 1A):**
$$\tau = P \times \lambda_m \times I = 11 \times 0.00611 \times 1.0 = \boxed{0.0671 \text{ Nm}}$$
This is the **theoretical torque** (from derived λm). For safety margin, use datasheet conservative value: **0.0934 Nm** (upper-bound estimate accounting for manufacturing tolerance and measurement conditions).

#### Step 3: Total Required Phase Voltage
$$V_{mag} = \sqrt{V_d^2 + V_q^2} = \sqrt{(-7.82)^2 + (24.33)^2} = \boxed{25.54 \text{ V}}$$

This is the **peak phase-to-neutral voltage** your inverter must produce.

#### Step 4: Map Back to DC Bus Voltage (Vdc) with 15% Transient Headroom

Using **Space Vector PWM (SVPWM)**:
$$V_{dc(min)} = V_{mag} \times \sqrt{3} = 25.54 \times 1.732 \approx 44.3 \text{ V}$$
$$V_{dc(with\ 15\%\ margin)} = 44.3 \times 1.15 \approx \boxed{51 \text{ V}}$$

Using **Sine-Triangle PWM**:
$$V_{dc(min)} = V_{mag} \times 2 = 25.54 \times 2 \approx 51.1 \text{ V}$$
$$V_{dc(with\ 15\%\ margin)} = 51.1 \times 1.15 \approx \boxed{59 \text{ V}}$$

### **Conclusion:**
**To operate at 2262 RPM average speed with 1A rated load and 15% transient safety margin, provision Vdc = 51-59V.** SVPWM allows 51V (practical 5S battery), Sine-PWM requires 59V (custom supply). Transient margin accommodates sudden load steps and control loop dynamics.

## PWM Modulation Techniques: SVPWM vs. Sine-Triangle

The relationship between your DC bus voltage (Vdc) and the maximum voltage you can output to the motor depends critically on which PWM scheme your inverter implements. This section derives the voltage ceiling for both common techniques.

### Background: The Modulation Limit

A voltage-source inverter can produce a sinusoidal output voltage up to a maximum magnitude determined by the DC bus. Three-phase PWM can be implemented in different ways:
- **SVPWM (Space Vector PWM):** Uses the full hexagonal voltage space→higher utilization
- **Sine-Triangle PWM:** Compares a sine reference against a triangular carrier→lower utilization

Both produce the same motor line-to-line voltages at steady-state, but their **maximum amplitude limits differ**.

### SVPWM (Space Vector PWM) — High Utilization 

**Why SVPWM?**
SVPWM synthesizes reference voltages using the six discrete voltage vectors of a three-phase inverter. By carefully dwell-timing between adjacent vectors, it can achieve any voltage magnitude inside a hexagon. This hexagon has radius:

$$V_{max,SVPWM} = \frac{V_{dc}}{\sqrt{3}}$$

**Derivation:**
The three phase-to-neutral peak voltages are constrained by:
$$|V_{an}|, |V_{bn}|, |V_{cn}| \leq \frac{V_{dc}}{2}$$

For a balanced three-phase system where $V_{an} + V_{bn} + V_{cn} = 0$, the maximum d-q magnitude is:
$$V_{max,SVPWM} = \frac{V_{dc}}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{V_{dc} \times \sqrt{3}}{3}$$

**Voltage Circle Constraint:**
Your d-q controller outputs must satisfy:
$$\sqrt{V_d^2 + V_q^2} \leq \frac{V_{dc}}{\sqrt{3}}$$

**For Your 51V System:**
$$V_{max,SVPWM} = \frac{51}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{51}{1.732} \approx 29.4 \text{ V}$$

This matches your saturation limits! Your operating point at 2262 RPM (Vq=24.33V, Vd=-7.82V, Vmag=25.54V) lies comfortably within this 29.4V circle.

### Sine-Triangle PWM — Lower Utilization

**How Sine-Triangle Works:**
The controller generates a sine wave reference $V_{ref}(t) = V_{peak} \sin(\omega t)$ and compares it against a triangular carrier signal at frequency $f_{sw}$ (e.g., 20 kHz). When $V_{ref} >$ carrier, the top switch is ON; otherwise, the bottom switch turns ON.

This produces PWM pulses that approximate the sine wave. However, the linear modulation region is limited.

**Maximum Voltage:**
For sine-triangle modulation with a symmetric two-level inverter, the maximum peak output voltage is:

$$V_{max,SineTri} = \frac{V_{dc}}{2}$$

**Why is it half?**
The triangular carrier spans from $-V_{ref,max}$ to $+V_{ref,max}$. When the sine reference peaks at $+V_{ref,max}$ (e.g., 100% modulation depth), the output voltage reaches:
$$V_{ph-n,peak} = \frac{V_{dc}}{2}$$

In balanced three-phase, the d-q magnitude is:
$$V_{max,SineTri} = \frac{V_{dc}}{2}$$

**For Your 59V System (if using Sine-Triangle):**
$$V_{max,SineTri} = \frac{59}{2} = 29.5 \text{ V}$$

(Approximately the same as SVPWM at 51V, since $59/2 ≈ 51/\sqrt{3}$.)

### Practical Comparison Table

| Metric | SVPWM | Sine-Triangle PWM |
|--------|-------|-------------------|
| **Max d-q Voltage** | $V_{max} = \frac{V_{dc}}{\sqrt{3}}$ | $V_{max} = \frac{V_{dc}}{2}$ |
| **Efficiency** | Higher (uses 15% more voltage space) | Lower |
| **Implementation** | Complex (requires modulation algorithm) | Simple (just compare sine vs. triangle) |
| **For Vdc = 51V** | $V_{max} = 29.4$ V | $V_{max} = 25.5$ V (not sufficient for your 25.54V point!) |
| **For Vdc = 59V** | $V_{max} = 34.1$ V | $V_{max} = 29.5$ V (just fits 25.54V) |
| **Your Application** | ✅ **Use SVPWM at 51V** | ❌ Would require 59V+ (inefficient) |

### Why Your System Uses SVPWM

With SVPWM and 51V DC bus:
$$V_{max} = \frac{51}{\sqrt{3}} = 29.4 \text{ V}$$

Your worst-case requirement (Vmag = 25.54V) leaves **3.9V of headroom** for transients—exactly your 15% margin.

If you had switched to sine-triangle PWM:
$$V_{max} = \frac{51}{2} = 25.5 \text{ V}$$

Your 25.54V operating point would **exceed the limit** (saturation immediately at rated conditions). You'd be forced to provision 59V instead.

### Controller Saturation Limits (From Modulation Constraints)

The PWM modulation ceiling directly translates to your d-q axis current controller output limits:

**SVPWM with Vdc = 51V:**
$$V_{max} = \frac{51}{\sqrt{3}} = 29.4 \text{ V}$$
$$\boxed{-29.4 \text{ V} \leq V_d \leq +29.4 \text{ V}}$$
$$\boxed{-29.4 \text{ V} \leq V_q \leq +29.4 \text{ V}}$$
$$\text{Constraint: } \sqrt{V_d^2 + V_q^2} \leq 29.4 \text{ V}$$

**Sine-Triangle PWM with Vdc = 51V:**
$$V_{max} = \frac{51}{2} = 25.5 \text{ V}$$
$$\boxed{-25.5 \text{ V} \leq V_d \leq +25.5 \text{ V}}$$
$$\boxed{-25.5 \text{ V} \leq V_q \leq +25.5 \text{ V}}$$
$$\text{Constraint: } \sqrt{V_d^2 + V_q^2} \leq 25.5 \text{ V}$$
⚠️ **Problem:** Your operating point requires 25.54V—**saturation occurs immediately at rated conditions!**

**Sine-Triangle PWM with Vdc = 59V (to Match SVPWM at 51V):**
$$V_{max} = \frac{59}{2} = 29.5 \text{ V}$$
$$\boxed{-29.5 \text{ V} \leq V_d \leq +29.5 \text{ V}}$$
$$\boxed{-29.5 \text{ V} \leq V_q \leq +29.5 \text{ V}}$$
$$\text{Constraint: } \sqrt{V_d^2 + V_q^2} \leq 29.5 \text{ V}$$

**Voltage Utilization Comparison:**

| Modulation | Vdc Required | Vmax Available | Your Need (25.54V) | Result |
|-----------|---|---|---|---|
| **SVPWM** | **51V** | **29.4V** | ✅ 3.9V headroom | **SELECTED** |
| **Sine-Triangle** | **51V** | **25.5V** | ❌ Saturates immediately | **Unacceptable** |
| **Sine-Triangle** | **59V** | **29.5V** | ✅ 3.9V headroom | **Inefficient (16% more Vdc)** |

**Key Insight:** SVPWM achieves **15% higher voltage utilization**, meaning you can reach the same performance with 51V instead of 59V. This saves cost and complexity.

**First Quadrant Operation (Your Scope):**
$$\boxed{-29.4 \text{ V} \leq V_d \leq 0 \text{ V}} \quad \text{(flux management, typically negative)}$$
$$\boxed{0 \text{ V} \leq V_q \leq 29.4 \text{ V}} \quad \text{(torque production, positive only)}$$

### Implementation in Firmware

Your d-q PI controller outputs must be saturated **before** the PWM modulator to prevent overmodulation:

```c
// Saturate to voltage circle boundary
float Vd_cmd = Vd_PI_output;
float Vq_cmd = Vq_PI_output;

float V_mag = sqrt(Vd_cmd*Vd_cmd + Vq_cmd*Vq_cmd);
float V_max = 29.4f;  // For SVPWM with 51V Vdc

if (V_mag > V_max) {
    // Scale both axes proportionally to fit inside voltage circle
    float scale = V_max / V_mag;
    Vd_cmd *= scale;
    Vq_cmd *= scale;
    // Integrator anti-windup: reduce unless already saturated
}

// Send to PWM modulator
SVPWM_modulate(Vd_cmd, Vq_cmd, Vdc);
```

**Critical Notes:**
1. **Scale both axes together** (not independently) to preserve d-q decoupling
2. **Anti-windup:** When saturated, freeze or reduce the current loop integrator
3. **Monitor saturation flag** to detect when voltage budget is exhausted
4. For first quadrant, you'll rarely hit these limits below 2262 RPM @ 1A

### Saturation Behavior Under Modulation Limits

When your d-q voltage command exceeds $V_{max}$:

1. **SVPWM Case:** The modulator clips the voltage vector to the circle boundary. Your current controller loses authority, and the motor **decelerates** (cannot maintain requested current).
2. **Sine-Triangle Case:** The modulator clips each phase independently. You lose the symmetric d-q property, and control becomes non-linear and asymmetric.

**Mitigation:** Implement a **voltage saturation block** before the PWM modulator:
```
if sqrt(Vd^2 + Vq^2) > Vmax:
    scale_factor = Vmax / sqrt(Vd^2 + Vq^2)
    Vd_out = Vd * scale_factor
    Vq_out = Vq * scale_factor
```

This keeps the voltage command inside the linear modulation region and preserves the d-q decoupling.

## Cascade Control Architecture

The FOC system uses a **3-stage nested loop** structure:

### Stage 1: Speed Controller (Outer Loop)
- **Input:** Reference Speed vs. Actual Speed
- **Error:** $e_{rpm} = n_{ref} - n_{actual}$
- **Output:** Torque Command, $\tau_{req}$
- **Saturation:** Clamped to rated torque (0.0934 Nm) to prevent overcurrent

### Stage 2: Torque-to-Current Mapping (Algebraic)
- **Input:** Required Torque ($\tau_{req}$)
- **Calculation:** $i_q^{ref} = \frac{1.5 \times P \times \lambda_m}{\tau_{req}}$
- **Constraint:** id = 0 (Maximum Torque Per Ampere)
- **Output:** q-axis current reference

### Stage 3: Current Controller (Inner Loop)
- **Input:** Torque current error, $e_{i_q} = i_q^{ref} - i_q^{measured}$
- **Output:** q-axis voltage command, $V_q$
- **Saturation:** Must respect voltage budget limit = $V_{dc} / \sqrt{3}$

#### Why This Stacking Works:
Each outer loop treats the inner loop as its "servant." The **Speed PI** doesn't care how it accomplishes torque; it just commands it. The **Current PI** doesn't care about speed; it just tries to achieve the requested current. This decoupling is why FOC is so robust.

## Cascade Control PI Tuning

### Current Loop Design (Highest Bandwidth)

The current loop is a **first-order system** (plant: $G(s) = \frac{1}{L_{dq}s + R_{dq}}$, using **amplitude-invariant 2/3 scaling** with $L_{dq} = 3$ mH and $R_{dq} = 8.4$ Ω).

For a 2000 Hz bandwidth and pole-zero cancellation:

$$K_p = L_{dq} \times \omega_{bw} = 0.003 \times 2\pi \times 2000 \approx 37.7$$

$$K_i = R_{dq} \times \omega_{bw} = 8.4 \times 2\pi \times 2000 \approx 105,558$$

**Critical Note:** These gains use **d-q frame inductance L_dq = 3 mH** (from 3/2 scaling of phase inductance 2 mH). If you switch to √(2/3) power-invariant transform, then use $L_d = 2$ mH directly and recalculate Kp = 0.002 × 2π × 2000 ≈ 25.1.

### Speed Loop Design (Lower Bandwidth)

The speed loop must be at least 10×slower than the current loop to ensure:
- Stable cascade behavior
- No oscillation or ringing
- Predictable transient response

Typical target: **200 Hz** (10× slower than the 2000 Hz current loop).

### Control Sampling & Timing Hierarchy

The complete timing cascade for your FOC system:

| Control Loop | Sampling Rate | Bandwidth | Ratio to Parent | Purpose |
|--------------|---------------|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| **PWM Modulation** | 20 kHz | — | — | Inverter switching (system clock) |
| **Current Loop** (Inner) | 20 kHz | 2 kHz | 1:1 with PWM | d-q current tracking, torque control |
| **Speed Loop** (Middle) | 2 kHz | 200 Hz | 1:10 of current | RPM regulation, cascaded from current |
| **Position Loop** (Outer) | 200 Hz | 20 Hz | 1:10 of speed | Angle/trajectory tracking (future) |

**Key Design Principles:**
1. **PWM operates at highest frequency (20 kHz):** Inverter switches at 20 kHz fundamental rate (independent of control loops)
2. **Current loop runs every PWM cycle:** Samples at 20 kHz, but with 2 kHz bandwidth (averaging multiple samples)
3. **Speed loop runs 10× slower:** Samples current loop output every 10 PWM cycles = 2 kHz sampling rate, 200 Hz bandwidth
4. **Position loop runs 10× slower again:** Samples speed loop every 200 Hz = future for trajectory control
5. **10× decoupling rule:** Each outer loop is 10× slower than its inner loop for stability

**Why 10× Decoupling?**
- Prevents aliasing and instability
- Inner loops settle before outer loops command changes
- Digital control stability margin (Nyquist criterion)
- Allows inner loop to reject disturbances before outer loop responds

**Timing Example (at 2262 RPM):**
- PWM period: 50 µs (20 kHz)
- Current loop update: Every 50 µs (can process ~100 samples per speed loop cycle)
- Speed loop update: Every 500 µs (20 PWM cycles)
- Position loop update: Every 5 ms (10 speed cycles)

## Voltage Limiting Strategy

### The "Voltage Circle" Rule in FOC

Your two PI controllers (d-axis and q-axis) both draw from the same DC bus "bank account":
$$V_{total} = \sqrt{V_d^2 + V_q^2} \leq V_{max}$$

Where:
$$V_{max} = \frac{V_{dc}}{\sqrt{3}} \quad \text{(for SVPWM)}$$

### Implementation (Recommended: Dynamic Saturation)

1. **Priority to d-axis (Flux Stability):**
   - Saturate $V_d$ output to $\pm V_{max}$

2. **Remaining Budget for q-axis (Torque):**
   - Calculate: $V_q^{max} = \sqrt{V_{max}^2 - V_d^2}$

## Lessons Learned

### Lesson 1: Control Stacking and Saturation Limits

**The Problem:** Without saturation limits, the Speed PI controller will "wind up" if the motor cannot accelerate fast enough (e.g., during stall). This produces massive current spikes that can destroy MOSFETs or blow shunt resistors.

**The Solution:** Implement **Software Fuse Protection** by limiting the speed loop output to the motor's rated torque (or equivalent current):
- Current Limit: 1.0 A
- Torque Limit: 0.0934 Nm (equivalent)

**Key Implementation Detail:** Ensure **Anti-Windup** is enabled. Standard methods:
- **Back-Calculation:** When saturated, reduce the integrator output proportionally
- **Clamping:** Freeze the integrator when the error reverses

**Why This Matters on XMC4700:**
Without these limits, transient events (sudden loads, startup spikes) would either:
1. Trigger an Overcurrent Trip (shutting down the motor)
2. Blow SMD Shunt Resistors or Power MOSFETs on your shield

### Lesson 2: The Forward Park/Clarke Transform is Not Optional

**The "White Box" vs. "Black Box" Gap:**

In your **Simulink simulation**, the motor block is a glass box. You can directly access the internal d-q currents ($i_d$, $i_q$) as state variables.

In your **XMC4700 hardware**, the motor is a black box. All you can measure are the three phase currents ($i_a$, $i_b$, $i_c$) from the shunt resistors.

**The Implication:** You MUST implement the Forward Park-Clarke Transform in your C-code to reconstruct the internal ($i_d$, $i_q$) from the measured phase currents. This is non-negotiable for real hardware.

**Simulation Advantage:** Your white-box model is faster and numerically stable, making it ideal for proving your PI gains are correct before committing to hardware.

### Lesson 3: High Stator Resistance Demands Careful Tuning

Your motor's **5.6Ω per-phase resistance (8.4Ω d-q equivalent) is unusually high** (typical motors: 0.5-2Ω). This has three critical implications:

1. **Large IR Voltage Drop:** At 1A, you lose 8.4V just to heating—a massive "voltage tax."
2. **Aggressive Ki Required:** Your PI integrator must be large to overcome this drop (Ki ≈ 105,558), but this makes the system prone to windup.
3. **Temperature Sensitivity:** As the motor heats up, resistance increases further, requiring adaptive gain tuning for long-duration runs.

**Mitigation:** Use back-calculation anti-windup and consider adding a series inductor to "smooth out" the high-R transients in future iterations.

### Lesson 4: Electrical Frequency ≠ Mechanical RPM

For a motor with **P = 11 pole pairs**, the relationship is:
$$f_e = \frac{60 \times n_{RPM} \times P}{60} = n_{RPM} \times P \times \frac{\pi}{30}$$

At 300 RPM mechanical, the electrical frequency is:
$$f_e = 300 \times 11 = 3300 \text{ pole pairs per minute} \rightarrow 55 \text{ Hz}$$

This is critical because:
- The Back-EMF depends on **electrical** frequency, not mechanical RPM
- The PWM switching pattern must align with this electrical frequency
- High pole count motors have much higher electrical frequencies than low-pole motors at the same RPM

**Verification:** After simulation, inspect the Va, Vb, Vc waveforms. They should oscillate at the calculated electrical frequency, not the mechanical RPM.

### Lesson 5: Understanding the "Voltage Wall"

As motor speed increases, Back-EMF increases linearly:
$$V_{back-EMF} = \omega_e \times \lambda_m$$

At some point, Back-EMF + IR drop equals your available voltage. Beyond this point, **acceleration becomes impossible** no matter how hard your controller tries.

This is the "Voltage Wall"—a hard physical limit defined by:
$$n_{max} = \frac{V_{available} - (K \times i_q \times R)}{K_v}$$

Where $K_v$ is the speed constant (RPM/V).

**For Your System:** With a 12V battery, this wall appears around 905 RPM (using passive physical resistance R_phase). With a 46V battery under active FOC control, it moves to the theoretical 1560 RPM limit.

### Lesson 6: Rated Voltage is a Design Point, Not a Hard Limit

The "12V Rated" specification on your motor datasheet is **not a physical constraint**—it is a **design operating point**.

**What it really means:**
- The motor was tested and characterized at 12V nominal (3S LiPo)
- Efficiency, torque, and speed curves are specified at this voltage
- 12V is convenient for battery-powered applications (not a limit)

**The actual physical constraints:**
1. **Insulation Voltage Class:** A 12V motor typically has 30-50V insulation rating (standard for small BLDC/PMSM motors). You can safely apply 30V+ AC to the windings.
2. **Current Rating (1A):** This IS hard—dictated by copper wire cross-section and thermal limits. Exceeding causes $I^2R$ heating and insulation failure.
3. **Mechanical Limits:** Bearing friction, rotor inertia, shaft stress (not electrical).

**With FOC + Current Limiting:**

If you command $i_q \leq 1$ A, you can apply **any voltage** to achieve any speed up to mechanical limits. The current loop protects the windings regardless of applied voltage.

Example:
- At 12V, motor reaches 585 RPM (loaded) due to Back-EMF + IR drop
- At 30V, same 1A current reaches 1560 RPM (same electrical stresses, higher speed)
- At 100V, same 1A current reaches 5200 RPM (if mechanically capable)

**For your resume/documentation:**
> "Recognized that motor 'rated voltage' is a design specification, not a physical limit. Under FOC current control, the system's electrical constraints are determined solely by current rating (1A), enabling operation across a wide voltage spectrum while maintaining identical thermal and magnetic stresses on the motor windings."

### Lesson 7: "Rated Speed" Becomes Obsolete with FOC

**The Paradigm Shift:**

In traditional fixed-voltage motor control, "Rated Speed" makes sense:
- Plug in 12V → motor spins at 585 RPM (fixed relationship)

In **Field Oriented Control with a variable inverter**, "Rated Speed" disappears:
- Speed becomes a free variable: $n = K_v \times (V_{dc} - I \times R)$
- You control speed by commanding voltage, not by waiting for Back-EMF to settle
- Current is independently clamped at 1A (your thermal limit)

**The Key Insight:**

With sufficient DC bus voltage and proper FOC implementation:

$$\text{You can run: } \boxed{n_{max} \text{ at } I_{max} \text{ at } \tau_{max}}$$

All three simultaneously. This is impossible with fixed-voltage drives.

**Example for your motor (using passive physical resistance R_phase=5.6Ω):**
- **12V (passive):** 905 RPM @ 1A (passive voltage application)
- **16V (datasheet point):** 1474 RPM @ 1A (at rated no-load condition)
- **46V (active FOC):** 1560 RPM @ 1A (with active control, current-limited, not voltage-limited)

*Note: The 46V case is achieved through active FOC control and can command the motor to any speed up to 1560 RPM by adjusting the control law, independent of the passive "voltage wall" formula.*

The motor **never "knows"** what voltage is applied. It only "feels" the current you demand. The voltage is just the means to push that current through the resistance and Back-EMF.

**Why this matters for your design:**

1. **Speed is not a rated parameter anymore—it's an operational choice.**
2. **The only hard limit is current (1A thermal rating).**
3. **Voltage becomes a "control knob," not a constraint.**

**For your resume (Senior-Level Insight):**
> "Recognized that Field Oriented Control eliminates the traditional concept of 'motor rated speed.' Under FOC with current limiting, speed becomes a free variable controlled by the DC bus voltage, while current remains independently clamped to thermal limits. This decoupling enables simultaneous maximum-speed, maximum-torque, and maximum-current operation within a sufficiently provisioned DC bus."

### Lesson 8: Transform Convention Determines Resistance Scaling

**The Problem:** The relationship between phase resistance ($R_{phase}$) and d-q frame resistance ($R_{dq}$) is **not arbitrary**—it depends on how you define the Park-Clarke transform.

**Transform Options:**
- **Option A (√(2/3) scaling):** Pure power-invariant; impedance unchanged: $R_{dq} = R_{phase} = 5.6$ Ω
- **Option B (2/3 scaling):** Amplitude-invariant (voltage magnitude preserved); requires: $R_{dq} = \frac{3}{2}R_{phase} = 8.4$ Ω

**Your Implementation:** Amplitude-invariant with 2/3 scaling:
$$[V_\alpha, V_\beta]^T = \frac{2}{3}[\text{Clarke}][V_a, V_b, V_c]^T$$
$$\sqrt{V_d^2 + V_q^2} = V_{peak}$$
$$P_{3phase} = \frac{3}{2}(V_d I_d + V_q I_q)$$

**Why the 3/2 factor appears:** The 2/3 scaling preserves voltage amplitude but modifies the impedance scaling. The 3/2 factor in the power equation is the mathematical consequence of this transform choice.

**Key Takeaway for Control:** All d-q frame calculations (voltage equations, PI gains, Vdc budget) must use $R_{dq} = 8.4$ Ω to be consistent with your amplitude-invariant 2/3 transform. Using $R_{phase}$ would mean you've silently switched to a different transform convention—leading to control law errors.

### Lesson 9: Maximum Torque Per Ampere (MTPA) for Non-Salient Motors

**The Problem:** With a current budget of 1A, how should you allocate it between d-axis (id) and q-axis (iq) currents to maximize torque?

**Theory:**
For non-salient motors (Ld = Lq), the torque equation simplifies to:
$$\tau = P \times \lambda_m \times i_q + (L_d - L_q) \times i_d \times i_q = P \times \lambda_m \times i_q$$

The second (reluctance) term vanishes because Ld = Lq.

**Key Insight:**
- **iq produces torque** (proportional to λm and flux linkage)
- **id produces zero torque** (no reluctance available) but costs voltage budget
- **Total current constraint:** $I_{total}^2 = i_d^2 + i_q^2 \leq 1^2$

**Optimal Strategy (MTPA):**
To maximize torque:
$$\tau_{max} = P \times \lambda_m \times i_{q,max}$$
This requires **id = 0** and **iq = 1A** uniquely.

**Your Implementation:** At 2262 RPM @ 1A, you correctly use MTPA:
$$i_d = 0 \quad \text{(no tortional requirement at this speed)}$$
$$i_q = 1 \text{ A} \quad \text{(100% of current budget to torque)}$$
$$\tau = 11 \times 0.00611 \times 1 = 0.0671 \text{ Nm}$$

**When id ≠ 0?** Only at very high speeds for **flux-weakening** (reducing back-EMF when back-EMF approaches Vdc limit). Your operating point is not yet in the flux-weakening regime.

**Key Takeaway:** MTPA control (id = 0 for non-salient motors) maximizes torque density and is the standard operating strategy below the flux-weakening threshold.

**For Your Firmware:** Document which transform you're using in the C-code. Future maintainers (or yourself in 6 months) need to know whether $R_{dq}$ or $R_{phase}$ applies in each calculation.

### Lesson 10: PWM Modulation Technique Selection and Voltage Utilization

**The Problem:** Two common PWM schemes can drive your motor—but they have different voltage ceilings. Which one should you choose?

**Background:**
Both SVPWM and Sine-Triangle generate three-phase sinusoidal outputs, but the **maximum amplitude they can produce differs**:
- **SVPWM (Space Vector PWM):** Uses the full hexagonal voltage space → higher utilization
- **Sine-Triangle PWM:** Clips each phase independently → lower utilization

**Key Formulas (Back-Calculated from Vdc):**

Given a DC bus voltage $V_{dc}$, the maximum output voltage magnitude is:

$$V_{max,SVPWM} = \frac{V_{dc}}{\sqrt{3}} \quad \text{(15% higher efficiency)}$$

$$V_{max,SineTri} = \frac{V_{dc}}{2} \quad \text{(traditional, lower utilization)}$$

**Numerical Comparison (for your motor requiring 25.54V):**

| Technique | Vdc Needed | Vmax Calc | Result |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **SVPWM** | **51V** | $51/\sqrt{3} = 29.4$ V | ✅ 3.9V headroom |
| **Sine-Triangle** | **51V** | $51/2 = 25.5$ V | ❌ Saturates (insufficient) |
| **Sine-Triangle** | **59V** | $59/2 = 29.5$ V | ✅ Works, but costs 16% more |

**Key Insight (Back-Calculation):**
Instead of asking "What voltage can I achieve with 51V?" (forward calculation), ask "What Vdc do I need for my required voltage?" (back-calculation):

$$\text{For SVPWM: } V_{dc,needed} = V_{required} \times \sqrt{3} = 25.54 \times 1.732 = 44.3 \text{ V} \quad (→ 51V with 15% margin)$$

$$\text{For Sine-Triangle: } V_{dc,needed} = V_{required} \times 2 = 25.54 \times 2 = 51.1 \text{ V} \quad (→ 59V with 15% margin)$$

**Decision Criteria:**
1. Is SVPWM modulation available on your inverter/microcontroller? (e.g., XMC microcontrollers often support it)
   - ✅ Yes → Use SVPWM, save 8V of Vdc budget and cost
   - ❌ No → Fall back to Sine-Triangle, accept higher Vdc requirement

2. How much does voltage cost in your application?
   - Low-cost: SVPWM saves ~$2-5 on battery cost (51V vs 59V)
   - High-reliability: Sine-Triangle is simpler to debug (fewer tuning parameters)

**Your Implementation:** SVPWM at 51V is the optimal choice—provides the same control authority as Sine-Triangle at 59V with **16% lower power budget**.

**Why This Matters in Firmware:**
- PWM scheme determines your $V_{max}$ saturation limit for the current controllers
- Incorrectly assuming Sine-Triangle limits (25.5V) when using SVPWM leaves 3.9V of unused voltage budget
- Conversely, assuming SVPWM limits (29.4V) with Sine-Triangle PWM leads to **overmodulation** and output distortion

**For Testing:** Simulate both modulation techniques in MATLAB/Simulink to visualize the voltage envelope difference and confirm saturation behavior at rated operating point.

### Lesson 11: Discrete Sampling Ripple in Speed Feedback → Modulation Artifacts

**The Problem (Discovered 01-04-2026):** In discrete MATLAB/Simulink simulation, motor speed is read from the plant model output only at discrete 2kHz sample times (not continuously). This creates discrete quantized steps in speed output → discrete steps in speed_error → discrete steps propagated through Speed PI output (Vq_req command). SVPWM modulator receives this rippled Vq_req (±1-2V ripple) and tries to "follow" it by modulating PWM rapidly, creating switching artifacts: **negative spikes in the positive half-cycle of inverter output**.

**Root Cause Chain:**
1. Continuous motor dynamics integrated in Simulink
2. Speed sampled @ 2kHz discrete sample times (not continuous)
3. Creates discrete quantized steps in speed output
4. Discrete steps propagate through Speed PI → Vq_req gets rippled (±1-2V)
5. SVPWM modulator receives rippled Vq and tries to follow ripple
6. Rapid switching pattern changes → **modulation artifacts (negative spikes)**

**Critical Misdiagnosis Initially:** This looked like a motor dynamics problem, inertia problem, or motor equation sign inversion. It was NOT. Purely a **modulation quality issue** caused by discrete sampling of continuous plant speed.

**Solution:** Add Discrete Transfer Function filter **between Speed PI output and SVPWM input** to smooth Vq_req before modulator sees it:
- **Numerator:** 0.01
- **Denominator:** [1 -0.99]
- **Sample Time:** 1/20000 (50 µs)
- **Pole location:** 0.99 (strong low-pass, ~160 Hz cutoff)
- **Phase lag @ 2kHz:** ~8° (acceptable stability margin)

**Result:** Eliminates negative spikes in inverter output, produces clean three-phase voltages, motor can now respond properly to speed commands.

**Why This Matters:** Discrete control systems introduce ripple that continuous systems don't have. The SVPWM modulator is designed to track smooth analog commands—when it receives quantized ripply commands, it misinterprets them as valid voltage requests and tries to follow them, creating unintended modulation artifacts. This is a **fundamental limitation of discrete speed sampling** in FOC systems and must be accounted for in production designs.

### Lesson 12: Discrete Transfer Function Filter Effectiveness and Startup Transient Ripple

**Discovery (01-04-2026):** Tested aggressive low-pass filtering with Discrete Transfer Function denominators [1 -0.90] and [1 -0.80] to attenuate discrete speed sampling ripple:

**Filter Configurations Tested:**
- **[1 -0.99]:** Pole at 0.99, ~32 Hz cutoff → Reduces ripple but leaves significant high-frequency component
- **[1 -0.90]:** Pole at 0.90, ~160 Hz cutoff → Better attenuation, ripple visibly reduced
- **[1 -0.80]:** Pole at 0.80, ~320 Hz cutoff → Most aggressive, but **startup transient still noisy**

**Vq Ripple During Transients (Lower Denominator = Stronger Filtering):**

![Filter [1 -0.90] - Vq Ripple Startup](image-1775013960379.png)
*Filter denominator [1 -0.90]: Moderate pole strength, startup ripple visible during acceleration*

![Filter [1 -0.80] - Vq Ripple Startup](image-1775014072950.png)
*Filter denominator [1 -0.80]: Most aggressive pole tested, startup transient remains noisy—even aggressive filtering cannot eliminate the non-stationary ripple*

**Key Finding:** Even with pole at 0.80 (320 Hz cutoff), startup phase (0-0.15s) exhibits **persistent ripple structure** on Vq_req during acceleration. This persists because:

1. **Startup is non-stationary:** Speed error (and therefore Speed PI output) changes rapidly during acceleration, creating a **bandwidth** problem, not just a frequency problem
2. **Filter assumes quasi-steady-state:** IIR filter designed for steady-state ripple rejection, but during startup the ripple "bandwidth" exceeds filter cutoff
3. **Trade-off discovered:** More aggressive pole (lower denominator value) reduces steady-state ripple but cannot eliminate startup ripple without sacrificing control bandwidth

**Steady-State Performance (After ~0.15s):** At steady state, all filter poles show significant ripple reduction. The aggressive [1 -0.80] filter produces smoothest steady-state Vq_req with ~0.2-0.3V ripple (down from ±1-2V unfiltered).

**Conclusion:** Frequency-domain filtering alone **cannot** eliminate discrete sampling ripple during startup transients because the problem is **time-varying**, not stationary. For production systems requiring clean startup, a **moving average** or state-dependent adaptive filter may be necessary to track the rapidly-changing ripple bandwidth during acceleration.

**Production Recommendation:** Use moving average buffer (50-tap FIR at 20 kHz PWM rate) instead of IIR to achieve time-domain averaging of discrete quantization steps, which handles both startup and steady-state ripple uniformly regardless of speed error rate of change.

   - Cutoff frequency: 2-5 kHz (much higher than speed loop)

### Why the Numbers in the Conversation Matter

1. **7.5Ω Resistance:** This is extremely high. It "taxes" your voltage budget heavily. Every 0.1A of current costs 0.75V just to overcome resistance. This is why high-resistance motors require oversized power supplies.

2. **11 Pole Pairs:** This causes electrical frequency to be 11× the mechanical frequency. It means high Back-EMF at reasonable speeds and tight timing constraints for PWM update rates in hardware.

3. **0.00545 Wb Flux:** This permanent magnet flux linkage determines the Back-EMF slope and torque constant. Stronger magnets = higher speed potential and torque, but also higher Back-EMF that limits top speed.

4. **1A Rated Current:** This is your thermal limit. Exceeding it for sustained periods causes $I^2 R$ heating (7.5W at full current!), melting insulation and destroying the motor.

### Transition to Hardware (XMC4700)

Once your simulation validates the control architecture, the path to hardware is:

1. **Park-Clarke Transforms (Hardware Feedback):**
   - Measure three phase currents: $i_a$, $i_b$, $i_c$
   - Implement Forward Clarke: $(i_\alpha, i_\beta) = f(i_a, i_b, i_c)$
   - Implement Forward Park: $(i_d, i_q) = f(i_\alpha, i_\beta, \theta_e)$
   - Feed these into your current PI controllers

2. **Angle Estimation ($\theta_e$):**
   - In simulation: Perfect angle from motor model
   - On hardware: Estimate from measured voltage angle or use a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL)

3. **Inverse Transforms (Hardware Output):**
   - Calculate $(V_d, V_q)$ from PI controllers
   - Implement Inverse Park: $(V_\alpha, V_\beta) = f(V_d, V_q, \theta_e)$
   - Implement Inverse Clarke: $(V_a, V_b, V_c) = f(V_\alpha, V_\beta)$
   - Feed into PWM/SVPWM generator

4. **PWM Generation:**
   - Use XMC4700's built-in PWM modules
   - Implement Space Vector PWM for maximum voltage utilization
   - Update frequency: 16-20 kHz (typical for motor drives)
